Why Diamond Grinding Is the Most Important Step in Any Epoxy Install

If there's one question we always ask a homeowner before quoting a job, it's this: "Has this floor ever been coated before, and if so, how was it prepped?" The answer tells us almost everything about whether the new coating is going to last.

Most epoxy failures we get called out to fix have nothing to do with the epoxy itself. They have to do with what happened — or didn't happen — to the concrete before the coating ever went down. And the single biggest difference between a floor that lasts 10+ years and one that starts peeling within 12 months is one step: diamond grinding.

Diamond grinding a recreation room floor before coating — Mid-Missouri project

What Diamond Grinding Actually Does

A diamond grinder is a heavy floor machine fitted with diamond-segmented discs that mechanically remove the top layer of the concrete surface. This does three things that no chemical etch or pressure wash can fully accomplish:

  • Removes contaminants — oil, sealers, old coatings, and efflorescence that sit in the pores of the concrete and prevent proper adhesion
  • Opens the concrete pores — creating a surface profile (think of it like roughing up wood before staining) that the epoxy can mechanically grip onto
  • Levels the surface — grinding down high spots, trowel marks, and minor imperfections so the finished floor is smoother and more even

Without this step, epoxy is essentially sitting on top of the concrete rather than bonding into it. It might look great on day one. But within months — sometimes weeks — you'll start to see peeling, bubbling, or hot tires lifting the coating right off the slab.

What This Looks Like on a Real Job

Here's a look at the prep and install process from a recent project. Every job starts the same way — grinding the entire surface, vacuuming the dust, and inspecting for cracks or repairs before a single drop of epoxy goes down.

Vacuuming up grinding dust — a clean surface is critical for adhesion

Once the floor is ground and cleaned, the base coat goes down first. This is the layer that actually bonds to the concrete and holds everything else in place.

Spreading the base coat — this is what the flake and topcoat will bond to

Then Comes the Flake

While the base coat is still wet, our crew broadcasts the decorative flake by hand across the entire surface — usually working in pairs to make sure coverage is even and there are no thin spots.

Full broadcast flake application — a two-person job for even coverage

After the flake cures, we scrape off any loose material and vacuum the floor again before applying the final protective topcoat — usually a polyaspartic for faster cure times and better UV stability.

Applying the final topcoat with a squeegee for an even, level finish

"Called three companies. Show Me Epoxy was the only one that mentioned diamond grinding before I asked. That's how I knew they were the real deal." — Robert K., Lake of the Ozarks, MO

The Bottom Line

Diamond grinding adds time and labor to a job — which is exactly why some companies skip it or substitute a quick acid wash instead. It's also why DIY kits almost never include it; most homeowners don't own (or want to rent) a 100+ lb grinder and a HEPA-rated dust collector.

But it's the difference between an epoxy floor that's still flawless in ten years and one that needs to be redone in two. Every floor we install — residential or commercial — gets fully diamond ground before anything else happens. No exceptions.
